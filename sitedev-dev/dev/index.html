Kuwait: Past to Present — History

Map of Kuwait: Click on any image in the exhibit to see fuller view.

Modern Kuwait traces its origins to Bedouin tribes who migrated from the central Arabian desert in the 17th century. The tribes adapted to the arid, coastal environment and became seafaring people. Bedouin tribal structure and traditions provide the foundation for much of Kuwaiti culture, including contemporary social hierarchies, and the interpretive arts. Traditional music, dance and poetry are integral parts of Kuwaiti culture and are highly regarded forms of expression.

The British Empire was a dominant force in the Persian Gulf for over 200 years. From the time the US entered the Gulf in the mid-19th century until the end of World War II, US involvement in the region focused primarily upon developing commercial relationships. After World War II Britain’s stabilizing presence in the region waned.  A British economic crisis coupled with domestic politics forced a unilateral British pullout in 1971 leaving the US on its own to protect what in the 1950s President Eisenhower had designated an area of vital national interest.  From Kuwait’s independence in 1961 through 1990 Iraq repeatedly claimed Kuwait as part of Iraq, desiring to improve access to and control of the Persian Gulf.  Iraq’s 1990 attempt to annex Kuwait fundamentally changed the geopolitical and geostrategic balance of the region and irrevocably altered the US-Kuwaiti relationship.
